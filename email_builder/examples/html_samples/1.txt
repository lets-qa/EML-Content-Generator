        <h2>🎣 Phishing: Not the Fun Kind with a Cooler and a Lake</h2>
        <p>
          Phishing emails are like bad pickup lines — they’re awkward, suspicious, and usually start with “Dear Valued Customer.”
          The goal? To trick you into giving away your passwords, credit card numbers, or the name of your first pet (which, coincidentally, is also your bank security question).
        </p>
        <h3>How to Spot a Phish (No Fishing License Required)</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>Check the sender:</strong> If it’s from <em>support@amaz0n.biz</em>, that’s not Amazon — that’s someone who failed spelling class.</li>
          <li><strong>Hover over links:</strong> If the link says “PayPal” but points to <em>paypa1.ru</em>, congratulations, you’ve found a scammer.</li>
          <li><strong>Urgency is a red flag:</strong> “Act now or lose access!” is scammer-speak for “Please panic and click this.”</li>
        </ul>
        <h3>Best Practices</h3>
        <p>
          - Don’t click suspicious links.  
          - Verify requests through official channels.  
          - Remember: No legitimate company will ask for your password via email… unless it’s your cat, and even then, be suspicious.
        </p>
        <p style="font-size: 12px; color: #888888;">
          P.S. If you ever get an email from me asking for your Netflix password, it’s just because I’m tired of paying for my own account. Still, don’t give it to me.
        </p>
