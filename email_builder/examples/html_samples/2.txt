        <h2>🧼 Password Hygiene: Because “123456” is Basically a Welcome Mat</h2>
        <p>
          Password hygiene is like personal hygiene — if you neglect it, things get ugly fast.  
          And just like you (hopefully) don’t reuse the same toothbrush for 10 years, you shouldn’t reuse the same password for every account.
        </p>
        <h3>Signs Your Password Needs a Shower</h3>
        <ul>
          <li><strong>It’s short:</strong> If your password is shorter than a tweet, it’s too short.</li>
          <li><strong>It’s predictable:</strong> “Password1” is not clever. Neither is “P@ssw0rd.”</li>
          <li><strong>It’s reused:</strong> If one breach can unlock your email, bank, and fantasy football league, you’re doing it wrong.</li>
        </ul>
        <h3>Best Practices</h3>
        <p>
          - Use a password manager (because remembering 47 unique passwords is a job for robots).
          - Make passwords long, random, and unique.

          - Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) — it’s like a bouncer for your accounts.
        </p>
        <p style="font-size: 12px; color: #888888;">
          P.S. If your password is “ilovepizza” — same, but please change it.
        </p>
